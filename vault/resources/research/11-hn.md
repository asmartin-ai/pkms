# 11 — Hacker News Anecdotes (Sweep A)

Raw findings from the Sweep A community-anecdotes workflow. 20 findings; 6 sample-verified by an adversarial fact-checker, 0 failed.
Program: [[00-ground-truths]] - Synthesis target: [[10-synthesis]]

## Top takeaways

1. **Notes and tasks need opposite decay policies.** Rediscovering a 5-year-old idea is gold; rediscovering a 5-year-old undone task is poison (F5). Tasks should expire with built-in amnesty (bujo's blank page); knowledge persists and resurfaces.
2. **Zero-friction capture + zero-friction accumulation = backlog death spiral** (F6, from someone who worked in the to-do-app space). Capture must be frictionless; *persistence* should cost a small re-commit action.
3. **The capture bar is `win+n`:** sub-2-seconds, zero decisions, cursor already in a textbox (F4). The daily note as universal sink with structure derived later (F10) is the proven shape.
4. **Customization is a perfectionism trap** — Obsidian plugin tinkering and "building the perfect system" replaced actual work for multiple ADHD devs (F1, F2). Cap the knobs; ship opinionated.
5. **Capture's psychological job is closing the mental loop** ("dealt with, asynchronously" — F3). Duplicates are fine; dedupe is the machine's job.
6. **Write-only graveyards are the default outcome, not the exception** (F13, F14). Retrieval and resurfacing must be designed as a first-class loop or the vault is decoration.
7. **Spec against the worst state** — tools get used precisely when executive function is degraded (F17, 45-year veteran). And LLM-as-organizer over raw dumps is already working in the wild (F18, F19); mobile capture decides winners (F20).

## Findings

### F1. Diagnosed ADHD dev abandoned Obsidian because infinite plugin tinkering replaced actual work; switched to OneNote and stuck with it partly BECAUSE it cannot be tinkered with, and its freeform spatial canvas fits how he lays out thought.

> Had me until "Obsidian" - absolute worst thing ever for my ADHD. Hours spent tinkering ... I was on the verge of developing plugins before I realized: I wasn't getting anything done.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=38277342
- **Who:** deafpolygon, self-described ADHD, on 'Hacking ADHD: Strategies for the modern developer' (732-point thread), 2023-11-15
- **Tool/system:** Obsidian (abandoned), OneNote (survived)
- **Design implication:** Cap the customization surface of the PKMS deliberately. Every extensibility hook is a perfectionism trap for this user (his Notion died the same way). Ship an opinionated, finished-feeling core; make 'tweaking the system' structurally boring or impossible.
- **Research questions:** 1, 2

### F2. ADHD perfectionism manifests as building 'the perfect system' in Obsidian-class tools while forgetting the actual tasks; the fix was a tool (OneNote) with paste-anything capture and deliberately minimal organizational options, plus accepting imperfection.

> My inbuilt desire for perfectionism occasionally leads me to believe I can create "the perfect system!" ... what actually happens is that I spend a large amount of time working on the system and forgetting about the actual tasks.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=38278084
- **Who:** pch00, self-described perfectionist replying in agreement under an ADHD-dx commenter, 'Hacking ADHD' thread (732 points), 2023-11-15
- **Tool/system:** Obsidian (failure mode), OneNote (workable)
- **Design implication:** Exactly matches the user's stated Notion death. The system must be 'good enough by default' on day one — no schema decisions, no folder taxonomy to design. Treat any session spent improving the system rather than using it as a smell to surface.
- **Research questions:** 1

### F3. Longitudinal same-author arc: in 2023 he wrote that GTD/org-mode-style systems were 'yet another routine to fail at' because he lacked executive function to even start them (https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=36997977); by 2026 org-mode+org-gtd stuck. The mechanism that made it work: instant inbox dump marks a thought as 'dealt with, asynchronously' so it stops looping; duplicates are explicitly fine; next-day review doubles as a cheap idea filter. He says it took months to become consistent.

> The thought pops up, I dump it in the inbox. It is now "dealt with", asynchronously, and the thought can stop bouncing around my head. ... Sometimes I'm not sure, so I just write it down again, no biggie if it gets duplicated.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=46613820
- **Who:** mtlmtlmtlmtl, self-described ADHD, Emacs user; pair of comments 2023-08-04 and 2026-01-14 (latter on 'Ask HN: ADHD – How do you manage the constant stream of thoughts?', 124 points)
- **Tool/system:** org-mode + org-gtd inbox
- **Design implication:** The inbox's psychological job is closing the mental loop at capture time, not organizing. Accept duplicates silently (dedupe later, mechanically or via LLM). Budget a months-long consistency ramp; design the system to survive inconsistent early use rather than punishing it.
- **Research questions:** 1, 2, 3, 6

### F4. Concrete max activation energy for capture: one global hotkey (win+n into OneNote) or a wallet sticky note. Unlocking a phone and navigating to a notes app's writing screen is already over the friction threshold and the capture won't happen.

> Anything higher friction than that isn't gonna work, which is why I don't use a phone app. Getting to the writing screen in any notes app is too much.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=38282012
- **Who:** jabroni_salad, replying in ADHD working-memory subthread of 'Hacking ADHD' (732 points), 2023-11-15
- **Tool/system:** OneNote quick-note hotkey, paper stickies
- **Design implication:** Benchmark the PKMS capture path against win+n: zero-decision, sub-2-second, cursor-already-in-textbox. A `pkms new` CLI that asks for a title or location already fails this bar; a global hotkey or always-open dump target passes.
- **Research questions:** 3

### F5. Veteran org-mode user gave up on digital task management entirely after years (Todoist and Taskwarrior also failed) because GTD-style review discipline created an ever-growing stale-task list 'so associated with guilt and stress' it caused paralysis and periodic 'TODO bankruptcy'. Paper bullet journal works because each day starts blank and unimportant tasks silently disappear without conscious deletion. Crucially he distinguishes notes from tasks: rediscovering 5-year-old ideas is gold, rediscovering 5-year-old undone tasks is poison.

> Being able to forget tasks is crucial for me to remain focused, productive, and mentally stable. ... I've found great value in rediscovering old ideas 5 years later, whereas finding 5 year old undone tasks is rarely something I want.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=44873136
- **Who:** setopt, Emacs/org-mode user (no dx stated), on 'I tried every todo app and ended up with a .txt file', 2025-08-12
- **Tool/system:** org-mode (kept for notes), paper bullet journal (tasks)
- **Design implication:** Architecturally separate decay policies: knowledge notes are permanent and resurfacing them is a feature; tasks must expire by default (auto-fade after N days unless re-affirmed). Never show a count of overdue items. This also explains why his 2.5-year bullet journal worked: daily blank page = built-in amnesty.
- **Research questions:** 1, 5, 6

### F6. ADHD commenter who worked in the to-do-app space: the accumulating backlog of 'too important to quit, too intractable to progress' items is THE unsolved killer of all task trackers; no to-do app 'works' today, and paper bullet journal wins specifically because pen-and-paper friction mostly solves backlog accumulation.

> Solving the backlog problem is the holy grail of To-Do systems. ... Right now the thing that works best is a physical Bullet Journal. It works because of the friction of paper and pen.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=44878788
- **Who:** Aerbil313, self-described ADHD, claims professional experience in the to-do app space, 2025-08-12, '.txt file' thread
- **Tool/system:** to-do apps generally; physical bullet journal
- **Design implication:** Counterintuitive: zero-friction capture plus zero-friction accumulation = backlog death spiral. Capture should be frictionless but PERSISTENCE should have friction — items must cost something (a re-commit action, a migration step like bujo's) to stay alive in the active view.
- **Research questions:** 5, 6

### F7. Anti-shame micro-mechanic: when you finish a task that was never on the list, add it retroactively and check it off later, converting invisible work into closure instead of letting it 'poke at your self-worth'.

> Just add it but don't check it off. Then when you come back to the list later, you check it off. ... Otherwise, the finished task will slide into oblivion and poke at your self-worth along the way.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=45085393
- **Who:** bentt, on 'Notes on Managing ADHD' (633-point thread), 2025-08-31
- **Tool/system:** TODO lists generally
- **Design implication:** Support a one-keystroke 'log done thing' entry (done-list, not just todo-list) in the daily note. The system should celebrate recorded-after-the-fact work — closure is the reward that keeps an ADHD user returning to the tool.
- **Research questions:** 5

### F8. Visible task aging beats hidden timestamps: a script prepends one parenthesis per day to undone Todoist tasks — ')))))))))) respond to important email' — making staleness impossible to miss and acting as an out-of-sight-out-of-mind countermeasure (concept borrowed from intend.do's NotDones).

> A visual indicator for task age works wonders for me. ... As the parentheses accumulate it's very obvious what I'm behind on.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=45086888
- **Who:** leroux-cifer, on 'Notes on Managing ADHD' (633 points), 2025-08-31; shares his script github.com/leroux/todoscript
- **Tool/system:** Todoist + custom script; intend.do NotDones
- **Design implication:** The SQLite index already knows item age — render it as an ambient visual signal (growing marks, fading color) rather than a guilt-laden 'overdue 47 days' label. Combine with the decay policy above: age indicators for a few days, then amnesty.
- **Research questions:** 4, 5

### F9. The 'Captain's Log' pattern solves cold-resume on paused projects: an append-only, timestamped, at-least-weekly 1-2 sentence status line per project, where 'no updates' is a valid entry, so any project can be resumed after months by reading the last few lines.

> My goal is that zero friction is needed for to resume work on any project, no matter how long it was paused. ... "No updates" is a valid update.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=45086111
- **Who:** pflenker, on 'Notes on Managing ADHD' (633 points), 2025-08-31; maintains it via a recurring todo entry
- **Tool/system:** append-only per-project log
- **Design implication:** Add a per-project 'log line' affordance to the vault (one command appends a timestamped sentence to the project note). This directly attacks the user's 90%-done problem: projects die because re-entry cost is unknowable; a 3-line read makes re-entry cheap and shame-free.
- **Research questions:** 2, 5

### F10. User with 'clinically significant ADHD' found Logseq the only system that survived after trying 'a lot': everything is thrown into the daily journal with liberal inline tags/links, and the knowledge network forms 'practically automatically' with no hierarchy to maintain.

> I just throw notes on my daily journal and add tags/links (same thing) liberally. The knowledge network is practically automatic and I don't fall into the trap of prescribing a hierarchical system.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=34138442
- **Who:** malnourish, stated clinically significant ADHD, 2022-12-26, 'Digital Gardening in Obsidian' thread; corroborated by non-ADHD bicx (id=43129166): 'if I have to organize or categorize entries... entries just don't get logged at all'
- **Tool/system:** Logseq (journal-first outliner)
- **Design implication:** Make the daily note the single default capture destination for everything; derive structure afterwards from wikilinks/tags via the indexer. Folders (projects/areas/resources) should be where curated notes LAND later, never a decision required at write time.
- **Research questions:** 2, 3

### F11. Self-described ADHD-brain commenter on schema-on-write: organizing at capture time adds exactly the cognitive load you cannot afford during an ADHD micro-distraction; but he also warns that fully automating organization just manufactures a bigger 'look at later' pile — scheduled queue-emptying is the critical, hardest part.

> I specifically DO NOT want extra cognitive load at that point. ... that automation will just create even more data for your "to look at later" pile.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=28263287
- **Who:** johnorourke, self-described 'ADHD-brain', on 'Schema on write is better to live by', 2021-08-22
- **Tool/system:** Obsidian; generic later-pile workflow
- **Design implication:** Tempering signal for the user's automation dream: LLM auto-extraction must not silently grow an unbounded organized backlog — it should also bound and surface the triage queue (e.g., fixed-size 'this week's reading', overflow auto-archives). The ritual of emptying matters more than the sorting.
- **Research questions:** 3, 6

### F12. Working triage architecture from a self-described information hoarder: separate purpose-specific dumps with built-in disposal — GitHub stars for code (then delete the link), Pocket for articles with periodic read-AND-purge, Obsidian reserved only for genuinely useful distilled knowledge; plus a weekly phone-tabs-to-laptop purge ritual because capture happens on the phone but sorting is only viable on desktop.

> The hard part is giving myself permission to delete articles which seem "timeless", but I don't stress over it.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=34273600
- **Who:** Moissanite, no dx stated, on 'My bad habit of hoarding information', 2023-01-06
- **Tool/system:** GitHub stars / Pocket / Obsidian split
- **Design implication:** For the saved-content backlog: keep the triage inbox as a separate stage from the vault, with an explicit promote-or-purge gate. Saved Reddit/HN posts live in the inbox; only after the deep-read do they earn a vault note. Permission-to-delete must be a designed-in default, not a willpower act.
- **Research questions:** 6

### F13. Obsidian as 'another red herring': a year of diligent note-taking chasing the Gwern-style ordered-notes dream produced notes he never read and that 'do nothing for me'; what survived was a zero-friction browser bookmarklet for quick notes plus a plain planner app (TickTick/Google Keep).

> I took a lot of notes, but I don't read them and they do nothing for me. I ended up after a lot of trial and error with a bookmarklet that lets me take notes right in a browser window.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=38276117
- **Who:** pwillia7, self-identifies with ADHD community, 'Hacking ADHD' thread (732 points), 2023-11-15
- **Tool/system:** Obsidian (abandoned), bookmarklet + TickTick/Google Keep (survived)
- **Design implication:** Write-only archives are the default failure state, not the exception. Retrieval/resurfacing must be designed as a first-class loop (search, backlinks surfaced at capture, scheduled resurfacing) or the vault is a graveyard regardless of capture quality.
- **Research questions:** 1, 4

### F14. ADHD dev with 'terrible memory retention' reached the opposite enlightenment after obsessing over note-taking: most digital notes are write-only, and their real flaw is that they never fade — he now deliberately uses forgetting as an aggressive filter ('if it's important, it'll come up again') and keeps only decades-scale memories in org-mode.

> Digitized notes are a good idea in theory, in practice most notes are write-only and are never read back ever again. ... That is the real problem of digital notes: they never fade away.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=39516871
- **Who:** sph, freelance developer, stated ADHD, on 'Notes on paper (2023)', 2024-02-26
- **Tool/system:** paper notebook + minimal org-mode
- **Design implication:** Build decay into the vault's attention model: un-touched captures should sink out of every default view (archive, not delete). The counterweight to the user's hoarding instinct is that an interface showing everything forever recreates the buried-Keep-notes problem he already has.
- **Research questions:** 4, 5

### F15. Recently-diagnosed ADHD dev names the 'new todo-app effect': the novelty of a new system IS the dopamine, so no productivity system works long-term — his adaptation is to accept and even enjoy switching systems every few months rather than fighting it.

> The point about the "new todo-app effect" is very real, and so I have now accepted that no productivity system will work long term and instead try to enjoy switching to new systems every few months.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=29196299
- **Who:** coagmano, diagnosed ADHD 2021, on 'Scattered thoughts on why I waste my own time', 2021-11-12; echoed by JohnBooty (id=38280690): 'it's ok to accept that one's systems will be ever-shifting'
- **Tool/system:** productivity systems generally
- **Design implication:** Design for churn-survival: plain markdown + regenerable SQLite already means front-ends can be rebuilt endlessly while data persists. Let the user scratch the novelty itch at the VIEW layer (new dashboards, new queries) while the capture substrate stays permanent and boring.
- **Research questions:** 1, 2

### F16. Responding to someone building an ADHD to-do app: the bottleneck is not list mechanics but the three emotions that derail task completion — excitement (about some other task), anxiety/perfectionism, and boredom; 'if you can solve these emotional issues then pretty much any todo list app (or just a notebook) will be fine.'

> I don't want an ADHD todo list app. I want something that to help manage my emotions while completing tasks.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=43430889
- **Who:** annie_muss, self-described ADHD (states it elsewhere, id=40498379), on 'Ask HN: I'm building a to-do app for ADHD', 2025-03-21
- **Tool/system:** ADHD to-do apps generally
- **Design implication:** Feature lists won't differentiate the PKMS; emotional ergonomics will. The excitement-hijack case maps directly to capture ('dump the washer-dryer research impulse here and return to taxes'); anxiety maps to shame-free defaults; boredom maps to making the next action tiny and concrete.
- **Research questions:** 1

### F17. 71-year-old ADHD+dyslexic engineer (45 years building systems) on why his own app failed: tools are designed assuming full executive function, but get used precisely when working memory, inhibition and sequencing are degraded — 'tools that rely on reflection or discipline stop working' exactly when needed most.

> I designed a tool that assumed users had full executive function available when they used it. They didn't.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47206316
- **Who:** capacity_guy, 71, ADHD/dyslexic, 45-year career mainframes-to-web, 2026-03-01 Show HN post
- **Tool/system:** his failed CBT-I app; productivity tooling generally
- **Design implication:** Spec the PKMS against the WORST state (med worn off, post-hyperfocus, 11pm), not the ideal one: every critical path (capture, find, resume) must work with near-zero working memory — no multi-step flows, no remembering where things go, no commands without discoverable prompts.
- **Research questions:** 1, 3

### F18. Probably-ADHD builder tried for years to maintain structured notes on Obsidian and other markdown tools and they 'never stuck'; what finally worked was talking into his phone, dumping quick fragments, and letting LLMs (Claude Code) extract the knowledge and tasks — journals, web clips and think-sessions all land in one place now.

> Structured notes never stuck for me. I just wanted to talk into my phone and dump quick fragments, then have something extract knowledge and tasks out of them. LLMs and Claude Code closed most of the gap.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47916617
- **Who:** tarr1124, 'probably ADHD', author of the OSS tool rill (self-promo caveat — but the years-long Obsidian abandonment story predates the product), 2026-04-27
- **Tool/system:** Obsidian (abandoned) → custom voice-dump + Claude Code extraction (rill.md)
- **Design implication:** Direct validation of the user's Google-Inbox/LLM instinct and his Notion postmortem ('without an LLM to help me digest... I got lost'). The viable division of labor: human does raw dumps (voice/text/clips), LLM does the structuring into the vault, indexer makes it queryable. Same architecture this PKMS is pursuing.
- **Research questions:** 3, 6

### F19. Engineer with 'quite extreme ADHD that even when treated' uses ChatGPT as the second brain his therapists prescribed — its distinct value over passive notes is active accountability: it asks whether he finished the last thing before letting him chase a new one, and breaks analysis paralysis by producing one concrete next step. In a later comment (id=47225584) he adds his capture layer took years to build, accepts voice+text from phone into Logseq/Trello/org-mode, and that LLM value lies 'more in retrieval than submission'.

> Now I have something to yell at me and ask if I actually finished the thing I said I would before jumping into some new thing. If I have not, it can give me a concrete next step.

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=43465835
- **Who:** theshackleford, stated extreme treated ADHD, therapist-advised second-brain user, on Goblin.tools thread, 2025-03-24
- **Tool/system:** ChatGPT as accountability layer over notes
- **Design implication:** An LLM layer on the vault shouldn't just summarize — it should close loops: on each session ask 'X was in progress, finished?' and emit exactly one next action. That's the 90%-done countermeasure, and matches the user's task-initiation-worst profile.
- **Research questions:** 4, 5

### F20. Engineer diagnosed at 38: Todoist became 'a complete game changer' where other systems (including 16 years of org-mode) failed — the deciding property was frictionless mobile capture; org-mode lost purely on the clunky mobile story. Corroborated in-thread by ash_091 (id=46611977, dx mid-30s): the trick is using it 'consistently, and for everything', seeding each morning with trivially completable routine items so the day 'is run by Todoist' instead of by impulse.

> Something about Todoist worked better for my brain (plus the mobile integration is awesome… my second best over the years was org-mode but the mobile story is way too clunky)

- **Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=46610770
- **Who:** tonyarkles, engineer, diagnosed ADHD at 38 (states dx in 45086362), on 'Ask HN: ADHD – constant stream of thoughts' (124 points), 2026-01-14
- **Tool/system:** Todoist (survived), org-mode (lost on mobile)
- **Design implication:** A desktop-only vault will lose the capture war the user has already settled with Keep/Discord. The PKMS needs a phone-side dump path (share-sheet, voice, or even keep piping through Discord/Keep into the inbox) from day one; treat desktop as the triage/reading surface, phone as the capture surface.
- **Research questions:** 2, 3
